# Pillar 4: Car R&D, Assembly, & Part Wear

## 1. Part Performance Attributes & Car Assembly
Every physical component designed and bolted onto the chassis operates as a discrete inventory item defined by 4 foundational technical attributes. The vehicle chassis utilizes 4 explicit, independent aerodynamic assembly slots: **Front Wing, Rear Wing, Underfloor, and Sidepods**.

### Core Component Attributes
* **Performance Points:** The raw numerical value that directly dictates the component's aerodynamic efficiency or mechanical downforce contribution to the baseline lap-time simulation engine.
* **Reliability %:** The temporary structural health pool. This percentage ticks down linearly with track mileage. Dropping below critical thresholds increases the probability of triggering minor faults or catastrophic failures via the Chaos Engine.
* **Weight Deviation & Structural Integrity Trade-off:** The physical mass of the component measured in kilograms relative to the regulations' optimal minimum limit. Lighter parts increase car acceleration and pace metrics but demand significantly higher upfront cash and premium manufacturing materials to fabricate. Players can manually adjust this variable during the manufacturing phase; choosing to "lightweight" a part shaves off mass to boost acceleration, but scales fabrication costs up by 50% and **inflicts a structural compromise—permanently penalizing both the component's starting reliability pool and its structural longevity decay rate**.
* **Max Condition Ceiling:** The permanent structural integrity limit of the part. This represents the maximum ceiling mechanics can repair the item to before permanent material fatigue sets in.

## 2. Component Inventory & Manufacturing Loop
Blueprints generated by the design staff are infinite, but physical parts are strictly independent items that must be explicitly queued, manufactured, and tracked in the database via unique component IDs.

### Factory Queue & Asymmetric Upgrades
* **Discrete Part Fabrication:** A player can choose to manufacture a single copy of a newly researched blueprint (e.g., "Spec-3 Front Wing"). 
* **Driver Spec Splitting:** The player can choose to bolt a singular new upgrade onto Car 1 while forcing Car 2 to run older component specifications. This allows the player to test single components or prioritize their lead driver when factory time or cash is constrained.
* **The Trackside Spares Cap & Emergency Procurement:** Teams are bound by a strict logistical crate limit when traveling to a race weekend (e.g., a maximum of 8 total spare component slots in the freight). Players must strategically choose which backup parts to pack, balancing the risk of crash damage against the reliability profiles of their current components. If a catastrophic multi-car crash occurs and a team has no matching packed spares remaining, they are forced to trigger **Emergency Procurement**. This automatically purchases an un-optimized, maximum-weight baseline part from the technical regulators for a harsh flat financial fine that **applies directly against their seasonal Cost Cap ledger**.

## 3. Dual-Layer Part Wear & Maintenance
Components experience two distinct types of degradation: temporary track wear that can be maintained trackside, and permanent material fatigue that leads to part death.

### The Degradation Layers
* **Current Reliability % (Dynamic Pool):** Starts at 100% (or lower if a lightweight trade-off was chosen) and drops linearly based on mileage completed across Practice, Qualifying, and the Race. Driving over aggressive curbs, operating in dirty air, or running high-intensity Pace Directives accelerates this wear. Mechanics can restore this pool back to the part's current maximum ceiling between sessions at the track.
* **Permanent Fatigue (Decay Ceiling):** Every time a component completes a full race weekend, its structural molecular integrity is compromised by heat, vibrations, and stress cycles, permanently shaving percentage points off its **Max Condition Ceiling** (e.g., dropping from a 100% maximum capacity down to 93% under standard conditions). This decay rate scales aggressively if the car suffers high-impact structural incidents on track, if the part was intentionally manufactured to be lightweight, or if the team operates with a low-tier **HQ Rig Testing facility**.
* **Scrapping and Cycle Life:** Once a component's permanent Max Condition Ceiling falls below a dangerous structural threshold (e.g., 60%), it becomes a critical reliability risk regardless of trackside repairs. The player is forced to decommission and scrap the physical part, forcing them to cycle a slot in the factory queue to build a fresh replacement.
